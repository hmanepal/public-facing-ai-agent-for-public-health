http://ees.elsevier.com/thelancet/www.thelancet.com   Vol 406   October 18, 2025  1723Ebola virus in Kasai 
revives 50-year-old questions on viral latency
On Sept 4, 2025,  DR Congo declared 
its 16th Ebola virus outbreak. The 
epicentre of this outbreak occurred in the Kasai province—the first resurgence in this region since 2008.
1 
Genomic analysis identified an Ebola virus with more than 99·5% identity to the lineage that was responsible for the first outbreak in Yambuku in 1976.
2 Instead of clustering with 
more recent outbreaks, a pathogen assumed to evolve steadily appears, in part, unchanged for half a century.
Antibodies and RNA fragments 
have been found in fruit bats, yet no reservoir species has yielded a live virus.
3 The Kasai strain’s proximity to 
the 1976 outbreak, therefore, narrows the plausible explanations of where, and under what circumstances, Ebola endures between epidemics.
One explanation is persistence, 
in which Ebola virus hibernates in tissue, alternating between replication and dormancy. This has not been shown in wild animals, but the virus has re-emerged in humans 5 years after apparent recovery.
4 Lassa virus 
provides an analogue, causing chronic infection in rodents with shedding that sustains transmission.
5 The Kasai 
sequences raise the possibility that Ebola persists similarly. 
A second explanation is slow 
circulation at a very low prevalence. Here, the virus never truly disappears but transmits so rarely that minimal transmission chains survive. With so few opportunities to replicate, it accumulates mutations glacially, as with the Marburg virus in fruit bats. However, the extraordinary stability of the Kasai strain pushes this model to its limits.
A third possibility is ecological 
equilibrium, in which the virus replicates continuously in hosts that tolerate infection without mounting a strong immune response. Mutations arise but rarely persist, creating the appearance of stability. This explanation  appears less convincing, 
as it requires a stable reservoir host and constant purging of mutations, which have yet to be supported by evidence.
Each explanation predicts distinct 
patterns: persistence would produce near-identical genomes from the same host populations years apart; slow circulation would show gradual genetic drift; and equilibrium would reveal year-round virus with limited diversity. The obstacle is not a lack of hypotheses, but the absence of systematic, long-term ecological fieldwork to test them.
The Kasai outbreak emphasises the 
case that genomic stasis might be a recurring feature of Ebola virus ecology. A lineage that is virtually unchanged since 1976 challenges assumptions about Ebola’s evolution and exposes the blind spots of surveillance. Successes with the Marburg and Bombali viruses show that a One Health approach of monitoring human survivors, wildlife, and ecological change together offers the best chance of detecting persistence before spillover. Until these mechanisms are understood, countermeasures will remain reactive, and Ebola virus will continue to resurface.
